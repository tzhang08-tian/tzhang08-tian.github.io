Monitoring a coral

< >

2026

Material: Arduino, Recycled cardboard, Tape, paper

Size: 15cm x 15cm x 30cm

Where is the boundary of the monitorable?
This is the question the work asks.
We touch it with sensors, translate it through screens, and predict it with algorithms.
When control and detection become forms of intimacy, does nature remain anything more than a sign?

We often say technology is advancing.
The world is developing.
But must development always accelerate?
Does progress carry a silent center from which everything is measured?
When everything becomes monitorable,
are we quietly redefining what is worthy of existence?
When progress is measured by our capacity to control,
are we beginning to treat what cannot be measured as invalid?

Technology grants us unprecedented visibility,
yet it also produces a new distance.
When experience is converted into data, when perception is replaced by models,
do we lose the ability to confront complexity and uncertainty directly?
We believe we are approaching the world more closely,
yet perhaps we are only approaching its simulation.

Green screen technology renders reality replaceable.
It allows environments to be erased and reconstructed into spaces more aligned with imagination and control.
In this work, the green screen is not merely a visual device,
but a reference to a technological logic—
when nature can be substituted, when the world becomes editable material,
do we gradually grow accustomed to understanding the real through simulation?
Technological development expands our capacity to construct,
yet it may also confine us within an increasingly processed world.

Published in Vision Magazine; exhibited at Brown University
May 2026, Providence, RI
